Shoujiki, pronounced (sho jee kee) is the Japanese word for honesty or integrity.

Integrity in The Shoujiki Method is not only honesty, but it’s also wholeness and strength, like the integrity of a bridge for example.

Included in the book is everything you need to play, just add your dice and timer. (A deluxe coffee table version with a hardwood case, playing cards, eight-sided belt colored dice and sand timer also available as a reward.) 

Using a martial arts theme, players graduate from white belt, with a very slim chance of controversy, on up to black belt, which has the highest chance of controversy. 

Players start with easy white belt questions then move through the ranks to more controversial questions. A Sensei or Teacher is appointed as a moderator at the start of the game. Sensei decides if and when players are in good humor and ready to advance up the ranks. Rank is as follows; white, yellow, green, blue, brown and black. Beginner's mind throughout the process means that players stay open, light-hearted and allow for differences to advance. 

Keeping a beginners mind, players allow for differences. The concept of beginner’s mind is common in martial arts. Not knowing anything, you’re open and ready to learn. When you already know everything, you are too filled up with no room for more information. In this game we assume nothing and set knowledge aside, opening up to other's opinions. 

In class black belts worked with real knives, so in higher belts the questions are meant to be edgy. Moving too quickly through the ranks is not recommended before knowing each other’s tolerances.


Benefits of The Shoujiki Method

A dojo is a place of martial arts practice. There, manners and etiquette are a priority so that the sensei can help students learn without injury. Wouldn’t it be nice if we all used dojo etiquette in conversation? 

What if people from diverse backgrounds gathering together entered a conversation with courtesy, like a bow of respect?

This martial arts theme supports cooperation and prevents escalating conflict. The Sensei moderates as players ask increasingly in depth questions. With a good sense of humor, players can move up the ranks. With interesting topic prompts through question cards, this game makes conversations enjoyable, safe, and meaningful. 

Friendly Chibi animals represent the lighthearted, child-like mood necessary to level up. The goal is to share honestly, while maintaining good humor, integrity, and compassion for differences. 

Players start by choosing a sensei (this should be someone easy-going). Sensei will act as the moderator and choose a question from the white belt cards. Sensei shows how it's done by answering the first question. Sensei also awards higher belts to players as they progress. As one advances, they keep their rank for the next game, but the questions still begin with a white belt lightness.

This method brings attention to how others perceive us and how we see the world around us by asking the right questions, but with a sense of humor as the focus.

I remember years ago when I was a blue belt, my sensei was training us and noticed the serious look on my face as I practiced. To keep my mood light, he said, “Do the movement again, but this time with your tongue hanging out to the side.” My partner and I tried our best to focus on technique without laughing. We also tried not to bite our tongues. 

The Shoujiki Method is designed to take us to the next level in our relationships. Just by listening to each other, we get a better idea of where we are, and where we want to go next. 

Often, throughout the process of this game creation, I wanted to give up. “It’s too hard, it will cause fights,” and so on. Then I would feel a spiritual tug on my heart to keep going. 

In today’s high-tech climate, there is something missing. So many distractions such as busy lives, phone checking, sharp and polarized opinions, and more are causing separations among us. We are awakening to the need to gather together in the flesh, in a safe environment of trust and connection.

 While social media keeps us connected to friends and family in some ways, The Shoujiki Method fills in the gaps of our craving for true relationship.

Conversational Balance

Balance is a problem in any group, so in our martial arts classes, we used a “talking stick.” Whoever had it would speak, and no one could interrupt. 

Here, the dice work like the talking stick. Whoever has the dice, has the floor, but the number of words they can use are limited by the number rolled on the dice. This encourages an even playing field where everyone speaks up, but only with a few words at a time. When someone has trouble articulating their thoughts and feelings in the right amount of words, this method encourages other players to join in. 

This is where your sense of humor guides the group, building a sense of mutual trust.

With the help of a moderator, or sensei, the players and their many diverse perspectives can be protected from judgment and conflict. There’s nothing like gathering around the table, picking up the benefits of genuine face-to-face interaction. 

Sitting with other people, there is a radiation of energy that becomes obvious with repeated practice. In class, we called this “the feeling.” As we approached our partners in technique, for example, we would sense their intentions. 

This feeling cannot be conveyed by phone, email, or social media. Becoming sensitive to it through noticing little things like micro-expressions or body language, is a strength. If you can do this while staying light, you’ll have an amazing conversational advantage. 

Listening with compassion for differences provides a mirror for other people to see and hear themselves more clearly. They may realize for the first time whether they come across as interesting or intolerant, for example.
