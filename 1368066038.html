What's so great about rare earth magnets?

Neodymium is part of a family of rare earth metals with special properties that can create astonishingly powerful magnets. These aren't refrigerator souvenir chachki magnets - these are jump out of your hand, can't separate from each other, handle with care magnets!  

What's not so great about rare earth magnets?

Although very powerful, neodymium magnets are fragile and can break when they crash into each other or into other hard objects at a distance. Also, rare earth metals are... you guessed it: rare. That means neodymium magnets are expensive and breaking makes it costly to experiment and play. Also, swvls, like any other devices that contain small, powerful magnets are appropriate for adults and children 14 years and up.

What are people asking about swvls (FAQ)?

Q: How long will the magnets in swvls last?

A: You'll have to ask your great, great grandchildren. Our neodymium magnets are permanent, rare-earth magnets that will last more than a lifetime. Specifically, we anticipate a reduction in strength of about 5% over 100 years. But remember, while neodymium magnets are very strong, they are also fragile and can break when two magnets collide (see video above) or when dropped from a height of several feet onto a hard surface. 

Q: Where are swvls made?

A: We do all of our manufacturing including injection molding, assembly, and quality control in the United States. We source materials internationally looking for the highest quality and consistency from our vendors. Currently, we import all of our neodymium magnets from a provider in China that has been our partner since the development of our first prototypes.

Q: Are swvls OK for kids?

A: swvls are appropriate for adults and children 14 years and up. While children enjoy playing with swvls and learning about the properties of magnets, parents should supervise their play and keep swvls out of reach of young children.

Q: What are the available colors for swvls?

A: We currently offer five colors: Black Hole, Red Magma, Ultraviolet, Ocean Blue, and Rainforest Green. Backers can choose any combination of colors through the survey we will send after funding.

Q: What's next?

A: We have lots of ideas, but are especially interested to hear from our backers on new colors (maybe Aurora Borealis glow in the dark?), ways to personalize swvls, and suggestions for experiments and school curriculum using swvls in science classes.

Risks and challenges

Neodymium and other rare earth magnets are not only very expensive, but also difficult to source. China is the primary commercial producer globally and although we have a reliable partner that supplies us with top quality magnets to our exact specifications, the ongoing trade war between the US and China could escalate further. Given the risk of increased costs due to tariffs, we have already built up our inventory ahead of the campaign and have sufficient materials on hand to manufacture and assemble 10,000 units.
